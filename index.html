Abstract

When exercising, many people assume that lifting weights and eating more automatically lead to muscle growth. While there is some truth to this, physics plays a major role in how efficiently these processes occur. Understanding these physical principles can improve both performance and safety. This article focuses on three key areas: energy balance through the Calories In / Calories Out (CICO) framework, the biomechanics of weightlifting, and how force production influences explosivity. The CICO section explains how individuals with different metabolic rates and thermic effects of food can respond differently to identical calorie targets (Hall et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2010). The biomechanics sections examine how forces and moments influence lifting difficulty, and how limb length changes the mechanical demands of movements such as the bench press (Escamilla, 2001). Finally, the explosivity section links resistance training adaptations to an increased rate of force development, described by fundamental physics principles (Aagaard et al., 2002). Together, these ideas demonstrate how physics underpins both technique and physical progress in weightlifting.

Calories In / Calories Out Debate

Calories In / Calories Out (CICO) describes energy balance in the body and is based on the conservation of energy: calories consumed must either be used, stored, or lost. Energy output includes basal metabolic rate (BMR), daily physical activity, and the thermic effect of food (TEF). While this framework is valid, individuals differ significantly in how many calories they actually expend (Hall et al., 2011).

The table illustrates three people consuming the same calorie intake but experiencing different usable energy due to differences in BMR and TEF. A trained individual typically has a higher BMR because muscle tissue is more metabolically active than fat, meaning they burn more calories at rest (Wang et al., 2010). This leaves fewer calories “left over” for weight gain compared to a sedentary person, who has a lower BMR and lower TEF. Someone with digestive inefficiencies may also absorb fewer calories overall, demonstrating that intake does not always equal usable energy.

These variations explain why two people following the same calorie target may not see the same progress. CICO remains a useful framework, but it should be treated as an approximation because metabolic rate, digestion, and body composition all influence how calories are processed. Practically, this means individuals should adjust their calorie intake if they are not losing or gaining weight as expected, rather than assuming the original calculation is universally accurate.

Bench Press Physics: Limb Length

[Explain how longer arms increase the moment at the shoulder and/or elbow:
- For the same barbell weight, a lifter with longer arms usually has a larger horizontal distance from the shoulder joint to the line of action of the load.
- Since the external moment is M = F × d⊥, a larger distance d⊥ means a larger moment that the shoulder muscles must counter (Escamilla, 2001).
- Longer arms also mean a greater vertical distance for the bar to travel, so more work is done: W = F × d. This helps explain why some lifters feel that bench press is harder despite using the same load as someone with shorter arms.

Training Explosivity (Rate of Force Development)

In physics terms, moving a weight explosively is about producing a large force on the bar in a short time. From Newton’s second law, F = ma, if a lifter can generate a higher peak force with their muscles, the same load can be given a larger acceleration. In practice this shows up as faster bar speed in a squat or bench press and a higher take-off velocity in a vertical jump. Resistance training does not just make muscles bigger; it can also increase the rate of force development through changes in neural drive and motor unit recruitment (Aagaard et al., 2002). This is why explosive exercises such as jumps, Olympic lift variations and fast concentric phases are often included when the goal is to improve power rather than only slow, maximal strength.
