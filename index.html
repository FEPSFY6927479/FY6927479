Calories In / Calories Out Debate

    
Calories in calories out is a framework that states calories in (from food sources) will be equal to the calories outputted by an individual in various ways, which can be for their daily activities, metabolic activity (“general energy for body upkeep”), thermic effect of food (“digesting food energy spent”). This principle follows the conservation of energy (energy can’t be created or destroyed), so excess calories not used as the body is stored as extra mass (fat or muscle) showing this principle obeys physics. However, the effective calories consumed by a person can differ between different individuals.
Demonstrated on the table are 3 individuals intaking the same calories with different BMR and different TEF (thermic effect of food). The trained individual has a higher BMR (which is dependent on the “weight” of a person) someone who is trained has more muscles  larger mass  larger BMR. In addition to this they experience a larger TEF this means there are less calories remaining for their BMR and any physical activities as compared to someone who is sedentary who would have more available. On the other hand, someone with a digestive inefficiency would also have less calories available after TEF meaning they would need extra calories to compensate for this loss. The examples show that people, even though they are following the same calorie goal, might experience different outcomes for their weight gain, loss or maintenance.
CICO arguably is still very useful because the body has so many factors involved in energy consumption, but it should still be acknowledged as an approximation. This shows when trying to gain or lose weight if no change occurs, then a change in calorie calculation is required (i.e. increase calories if not increasing weight and decrease if not losing weight). The table supports that there is biological variability across and no flaw in physics and this is the reason calorie responses vary between individuals. 
*It should be noted that when calculating BMR online it only considers “weight” this is crucial because overall muscle has a higher metabolic activity than fat (it burns more energy to have it) meaning your BMR could be higher or lower than expected, once again reinforcing that CICO itself is an approximation.
